Why it matters

When your IP and GPS
disagree.

Most apps only read your GPS, so a spoofed location just works. But a few also look at your IP address to see roughly which country you're connecting from — and if that country doesn't match your GPS, it can look inconsistent. Here's who actually needs to worry about it, and who doesn't.

A quick way to picture it

Your GPS is the pin on the map your phone reports. Your IP address is the network address you connect from, which roughly maps to a country. Most apps only care about the pin. A small number compare the pin to the network — and want both to point to the same part of the world.

Dating apps

Tinder, Bumble, Hinge and Grindr can compare your GPS to your IP. Matching both to the same country keeps your setup looking natural instead of mismatched.

Pokémon GO & location games

Location games like Pokémon GO can flag a GPS-versus-IP mismatch. Aligning your IP to the same country as your spoof keeps things consistent.

Most apps don't check

Life360, family sharing, Find My and the like read GPS only — they don't look at your IP. For those, changing your location is enough and a VPN isn't needed.

How Wander handles it

Two honest ways to line up your IP.

Wander itself doesn't run a VPN. What it does is help you align your IP so it points to the same country as your spoofed GPS. Aim for country-level matching — city-level IP geolocation is unreliable, so a same-country exit is the realistic goal.
